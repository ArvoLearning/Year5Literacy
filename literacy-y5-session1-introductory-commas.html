THE RULE — COMMA AFTER THE INTRO

Setting the scene before the main idea begins

⏱ 5–20 min

Quick review: An independent clause has a subject + verb and can stand alone. "I went home." is independent — it's complete by itself.

But sometimes we add a little "scene-setter" before it — a phrase or dependent clause that tells us when, where, why, or how. That scene-setter is the introductory element.

🛑 THE RULE: When a phrase or dependent clause comes at the beginning of a sentence (before the main idea), a comma usually follows it!
🚏 THREE TYPES OF INTRODUCTIONS — Tap to Explore

📍 PREPOSITIONAL PHRASE

In the morning, I ate breakfast.
Starts with a preposition (in, on, at, after, before, during…) + tells WHEN or WHERE.

🐾 ADVERBIAL PHRASE

Walking slowly, the dog sniffed the ground.
Starts with an -ing verb, describes HOW the action in the main clause happens.

🧩 DEPENDENT CLAUSE

Because it was raining, we stayed inside.
Has its own subject + verb but starts with a word like "because" — can't stand alone (remember complex sentences!).

🎧 The Pause Test: Read the sentence aloud. If you naturally pause for a breath after the first chunk of words, that's where your comma goes!

Try one of these starter chips, or invent your own:

In my opinion,
Walking down the street,

🧩 Sentence 2 — Start with an Introductory DEPENDENT CLAUSE

Try one of these starter chips, or invent your own:

Because I was tired,
When the bell rang,
Although it was cold,
If I finish my homework,
While we waited,
